An International Day for Euler's Number

A proposal to designate 21 February as a worldwide celebration of the mathematical constant e, timed to its tercentenary in 2027.

21 · II · 2027

Why e Deserves Its Day

Over the past three centuries, Euler's number e ≈ 2.71828 has emerged as one of the most important constants in modern mathematics, with an impact that rivals that of π. It is the base of the natural logarithm, the unique number for which the exponential function equals its own derivative, and a constant that appears throughout the mathematical sciences and engineering — in compound interest and population growth, probability theory and statistical mechanics, complex analysis, and the solutions of differential equations governing diffusion, catalysis, wave propagation, and quantum mechanics.

While the International Day of Mathematics on 14 March (Pi Day) has successfully brought π into the public imagination, no comparable formal recognition exists for e. Informal celebrations on 7 February (from the American date format 2/7) or 27 January (from the European 27/1) have arisen in some educational settings, but these are based solely on the decimal approximation and lack any institutional endorsement.

A Century of Near-Misses

The number e made its mark more than a century before receiving its formal designation. It appeared unnoticed in 1618 in an appendix to John Napier's work on logarithms. In 1683, Jakob Bernoulli encountered it as the limiting value of (1 + 1/n)n in his study of compound interest, but never isolated it as a named constant. In 1690, Gottfried Leibniz used the letter b for the number in correspondence with Christiaan Huygens. Roger Cotes, the editor of the second edition of Newton's Principia, rediscovered the same number in 1714 as a universal ratio connecting exponential and trigonometric functions — but his early death in 1716 left the insight undeveloped.

It took a twenty-one-year-old Leonhard Euler to finally unify these scattered encounters into a single mathematical object. In late 1727, in a manuscript on the explosive forces in cannons, he wrote: "For the number whose logarithm is unity, let e be written." This was the first recorded use of the letter e for the constant — the moment when the number became e.

Why 21 February

We propose that the date for e Day be grounded not in the decimal expansion of e, but in one of its most remarkable mathematical properties: the continued fraction representation.

e = [2; 1, 2, 1, 1, 4, 1, 1, 6, 1, 1, 8, …] The first four partial quotients encode 21/2 in European date format

Euler demonstrated that the continued fraction expansion of e follows the strikingly regular pattern [1, 2k, 1] for k = 1, 2, 3, … — a structure that is exceptional among fundamental constants. By contrast, the continued fraction of π reveals no discernible pattern. This expansion was central to Euler's proof that e is irrational, and the Padé approximants arising from it later provided the foundation for Hermite's 1873 proof that e is transcendental.

The first four partial quotients — 2, 1, 2, 1 — read in the European day/month format give the date 21/2, that is, 21 February. This encoding is philosophically distinct from Pi Day's approach: where 14 March encodes the decimal digits 3.14, a representation tied to the accident of base-ten notation, the date 21 February encodes the continued fraction of e — a representation that is intrinsic to the number itself, independent of any base.

The Tercentenary of Euler's Introduction of e

The Tercentenary

The year 2027 marks the three-hundredth anniversary of Euler's introduction of the letter e into mathematical notation. We propose that 21 February 2027 serve as the inaugural celebration — a tercentenary occasion of singular historical significance. Such an anniversary will not come again.
