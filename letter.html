Open Letter

A Proposal for the Designation of 21 February as the International Day of Euler's Number

To: Prof. Christoph Sorger, Secretary General, International Mathematical Union
cc: Prof. Ravi Vakil, President, American Mathematical Society; Prof. Joseph Silverman, President-Elect, AMS; Prof. Jan Philip Solovej, President, European Mathematical Society; The Euler Society
Date: April 2026

I write to respectfully propose that the International Mathematical Union consider designating 21 February as the International Day of Euler's Number (e Day), in recognition of the profound role that the mathematical constant e plays across mathematics, the sciences, and modern life.

The Case for Recognising e

The constant e ≈ 2.71828 stands alongside π as one of the most fundamental numbers in all of mathematics. It is the base of the natural logarithm, the unique number for which the exponential function equals its own derivative, and a constant that appears throughout the mathematical sciences: in compound interest and population growth, in probability theory and statistical mechanics, in the solutions of differential equations that govern phenomena from heat diffusion to quantum mechanics.

The number itself emerged gradually over more than a century before it received its name. It appeared implicitly in 1618 in an appendix to John Napier's work on logarithms, in a table likely computed by William Oughtred, though logarithms were not yet conceived in terms of a base and no constant was identified. In 1683, Jakob Bernoulli encountered the number as the limiting value of compound interest — showing that (1 + 1/n)n converges to a value between 2 and 3 — but he never isolated it as a named constant. Gottfried Leibniz used the letter b for the number in correspondence with Christiaan Huygens in 1690–1691. Roger Cotes, in work published posthumously in 1722, came close to establishing the number's role as a universal base connecting exponential and trigonometric functions, but his early death in 1716 at the age of thirty-three left the insight undeveloped.

It was Leonhard Euler who crystallised a century of scattered encounters into a single, unified mathematical object. In 1727, at the age of twenty-one, Euler assigned the constant its permanent symbol — the letter e — and from that moment forward, the number had an identity. In the years that followed, it was Euler who proved that e is irrational, who computed it to eighteen decimal places, who expressed it as an infinite series, who connected it to the trigonometric functions through his celebrated formula, and who placed it at the heart of analysis. The naming was the act that transformed a diffuse mathematical phenomenon into a recognised constant. It is this act — Euler's introduction of the letter e in 1727 — whose three-hundredth anniversary we now have the opportunity to celebrate.

While the International Day of Mathematics on 14 March (Pi Day) has successfully brought π into the public imagination, no comparable formal recognition exists for e. Informal celebrations of e Day on 7 February (from the American date format 2/7) or 27 January (from the European 27/1) have arisen in some educational settings, but these are based solely on the decimal approximation of e and lack any institutional endorsement. None has been recognised by a mathematical society, the IMU, or UNESCO.

Why 21 February

I propose that the date for e Day be grounded not in the decimal expansion of e but in one of its most remarkable mathematical properties: its continued fraction representation.

Euler demonstrated that the simple continued fraction expansion of e is [2; 1, 2, 1, 1, 4, 1, 1, 6, 1, 1, 8, …], exhibiting the strikingly regular pattern [1, 2k, 1] for k = 1, 2, 3, … after the initial term. This expansion was central to his proof that e is irrational — a landmark result in the history of number theory. The continued fraction expansion of e, with its elegant periodicity, is exceptional among fundamental constants: where the continued fraction of π reveals no discernible structure, that of e reflects the deep arithmetic nature of the constant. The Padé approximants that arise from it later provided the foundation for Hermite's 1873 proof that e is transcendental.

The first four partial quotients of this expansion are 2, 1, 2, 1. Read in the European day/month date format — befitting a constant whose theory was developed on the European continent — these encode the date 21/2, that is, 21 February.

This derivation is philosophically distinct from the approach that produced Pi Day. Where 14 March encodes the decimal digits 3.14 — a representation tied to the accident of base-ten notation — the date 21 February encodes the continued fraction of e, a representation that is intrinsic to the number itself, independent of any base. It connects the celebration directly to Euler's own mathematical contribution.

Calendar Considerations

I am aware that 21 February is observed as the International Mother Language Day, proclaimed by UNESCO in 1999. This proposal does not seek to displace that observance. Multiple international commemorations can and do share calendar dates. The recognition I seek is, in the first instance, from the mathematical community itself — through the IMU and its member societies — rather than through a separate UNESCO proclamation. Should the proposal gain sufficient support, the question of formal coexistence with International Mother Language Day could be addressed at a later stage.

Timing and Opportunity

In late 1727, the twenty-one-year-old Leonhard Euler composed a manuscript titled Meditatio in Experimenta explosione tormentorum nuper instituta ("Meditation on experiments made recently on the firing of cannon"), describing seven experiments performed between 21 August and 2 September 1727. In this manuscript, he wrote: "For the number whose logarithm is unity, let e be written." This was the first recorded use of the letter e for this constant. Euler subsequently used e in a letter to Christian Goldbach on 25 November 1731, and its first appearance in a printed publication was in his Mechanica of 1736.

The year 2027 therefore marks the three-hundredth anniversary of Euler's introduction of the letter e into mathematical notation. I propose that 21 February 2027 serve as the inaugural celebration of e Day — a tercentenary occasion of singular historical significance. Such an anniversary will not come again.

I note with respect that the IMU General Assembly will convene in New York in July 2026, and that the European Mathematical Society Council will meet in Lisbon in June 2026. Both occasions present natural opportunities for this proposal to be discussed. This letter is being shared in parallel with the leadership of the American Mathematical Society, the European Mathematical Society, and the Euler Society, in the hope of building broad support across the international mathematical community.

The Request

I respectfully ask that the IMU Executive Committee consider this proposal and, if it finds merit in the idea, take the following steps:

1. Acknowledge and endorse 21 February as a day to celebrate Euler's number e and its significance to mathematics.

2. Invite the IMU's member societies and Adhering Organisations to adopt and promote the observance.

3. Consider, at the appropriate time, whether to recommend 21 February for broader international recognition.

The International Day of Mathematics demonstrated that the mathematical community, led by the IMU, can bring a number to the world's attention in a way that inspires curiosity, celebrates intellectual heritage, and draws new audiences to our discipline. The constant e deserves the same.

I would welcome the opportunity to discuss this proposal further and to provide any additional material that might be helpful to the Executive Committee's consideration.
