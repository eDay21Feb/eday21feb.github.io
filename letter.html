e\(\rm -Day\) : 21st February

Open Letter

A proposal to designate 21st February as as International \(e\)-Day, a worldwide celebration of the mathematical constant \(e\), timed to its tercentenary in 2027.

To: Prof. Christoph Sorger, Secretary General, International Mathematical Union
cc: Prof. Ravi Vakil, President, American Mathematical Society; Prof. Joseph Silverman, President-Elect, AMS; Prof. Jan Philip Solovej, President, European Mathematical Society; Prof. Kazuhiro Ishige, President, Mathematical Society of Japan; The Euler Society; The Bernoulli-Euler Society, University of Basel
Date: 10 April 2026

As a high school student long fascinated by the elegance of Euler's number \(e \approx 2.71828\ldots\), I respectfully request that the International Mathematical Union consider designating 21st February as International \(e\)-Day, in recognition of the profound role that the mathematical constant plays in mathematics and the sciences.

The Case for Recognising \(e\)

Over the past three centuries, \(e\) has emerged as one of the most important constants in modern mathematics, with an impact that arguably equals that of \(\pi\). As the base of the exponential function that equals its own derivative and of the natural logarithm, it appears throughout the mathematical sciences and engineering: in finance and population growth, probability theory and statistical mechanics, complex analysis, artificial intelligence, and the solutions of differential equations governing diffusion, catalysis, wave theory, and quantum mechanics.

The history of \(e\) is well chronicled, but a brief recounting is helpful here. The number slipped unnoticed into Napier's work on logarithms in 1618, then appeared in 1683 in Bernoulli's work on compound growth as the limit of the growth factor \(\left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n\). Leibniz and then Cotes used calculus-based frameworks to establish a link between the infinite reciprocal factorial series and the natural base of logarithms, computing it to several decimal places. It was Euler who finally unified a century of these scattered efforts into a cohesive mathematical object. In his 1727 unpublished manuscript on the firing of cannons, the then 21-year-old Euler wrote: "For the number whose logarithm is unity, let \(e\) be written." This was the first recorded use of the letter \(e\) to denote the number. Over the next few decades, Euler gave \(e\) its own identity: he proved its irrationality, derived its infinite series, computed it to eighteen decimal places, and connected it to the trigonometric functions through the celebrated formula \(e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0\) that bears his name.

While Pi Day, celebrated on 14th March as the International Day of Mathematics, has successfully brought \(\pi\) into our cultural imagination, no comparable recognition exists for \(e\). Informal celebrations take place in educational settings based on its decimal approximation, 7th February (American 2/7) as well as 27th January (European 27/1), yet none of these efforts carry institutional endorsement from a mathematical society, the IMU, or UNESCO.

Why 21st February

The impact of \(e\) stems from its rich arithmetic nature and mathematical properties. For this reason, the date for \(e\)-Day should be grounded not in the decimal expansion of \(e\) but in one of its most remarkable properties: its continued fraction representation.

Euler himself showed that \(e\) has the non-terminating continued fraction expansion \([2;\; 1, 2, 1, 1, 4, 1, 1, 6, 1, 1, 8, \ldots]\). After the first term, the fraction is characterised by the pattern \([1, 2k, 1]\), for \(k = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\) The simple yet elegant periodicity of this expansion is exceptional among fundamental constants. The continued fraction of \(\pi\), by contrast, has no such structure. Euler's pattern was central to his own proof that \(e\) is irrational and later provided the foundation for Hermite's proof of its transcendence. These landmark results helped \(e\) become \(e\).

The proposed date for \(e\)-Day is based on the first three coefficients \(\mathbf{2, 1, 2}\) containing the integer part and the opening of the periodic structure that generates the fraction. Read in the European day/month format, the coefficients yield the date 21/2, or 21st February. The date is the smallest initial segment of Euler's infinite expansion that forms a complete day-and-month pair.

This basis for the date is fundamentally distinct from Pi Day. Rather than relying on a base-ten decimal representation and an American date format, the use of the continued fraction ensures that the date is intrinsic to the number itself, independent of any base. The European day/month format respects its European origins, celebrating the contribution of Euler and his predecessors in shaping our understanding of \(e\).

Timing and Opportunity

The year 2027 marks the three-hundredth anniversary of Euler's introduction of the letter \(e\) into mathematical notation, a tercentenary occasion of singular historical significance that will not come again. I propose that 21st February 2027 serve as the inaugural celebration of \(e\)-Day.

I am aware that the IMU General Assembly will convene in New York in July 2026, and the European Mathematical Society Council in Lisbon in June 2026. Both occasions are ideal for the introduction and discussion of this proposal. This timing allows the General Assembly to consider the proposal with sufficient lead time for an inaugural observance on 21st February 2027. The letter is being shared in parallel with the American Mathematical Society, the European Mathematical Society, the Mathematical Society of Japan, the Euler Society, and the Bernoulli-Euler Society in Basel, in the hope of building broad support across the international mathematical community. A dedicated website and open endorsement campaign will follow in the coming weeks, and I will share the consolidated list of signatories with the Executive Committee in advance of the General Assembly in July.

What \(e\)-Day Might Look Like

Pi Day owes its success in part to accumulated rituals such as recitations, classroom activities, and the obvious pun on pie. \(e\)-Day offers comparable possibilities with a distinctive character that honours its origins, properties, and myriad applications. An Euler Lectureship, hosted in rotation by member societies and broadcast worldwide, would give the day serious mathematical content, consistent with the mathematical depth of the constant. School and university programmes could connect \(e\) to topics of immediate and future relevance, including compound interest and financial literacy, exponential growth and public health, and the role of \(e\) in the machine learning systems now reshaping daily life. The continued fraction itself offers an unusually accessible entry point into deeper number theory for K–12 and college students. These are illustrative possibilities. A planning meeting convened by the societies could develop a fuller programme of outreach activities.

Calendar Considerations

I am aware that 21st February is also observed as International Mother Language Day, proclaimed by UNESCO in 1999. The two observances honour entirely different things, and there is ample precedent for international commemorations to share calendar dates that serve different communities without competition for meaning. The present proposal seeks recognition from the mathematical community, through the IMU and its member societies, and it does not require any action by UNESCO.

The Request

I respectfully ask the IMU Executive Committee to consider this proposal and, if it finds merit in the idea, to take the following steps:

1. Acknowledge and endorse 21st February as a day to celebrate Euler's number \(e\) and its significance in the mathematical sciences and engineering.

2. Invite the IMU's member societies and Adhering Organisations to adopt and promote the observance.

3. Consider, at the appropriate time, whether to recommend 21st February for broader international recognition through a UNESCO proclamation.

The International Day of Mathematics demonstrated that the mathematical community, led by the IMU, can bring a number to the world's attention in a way that celebrates intellectual heritage, inspires curiosity, and draws new audiences. Given its relevance in modern life, the constant \(e\) is fully capable of doing the same and deserves a dedicated day in its own right.

I would welcome the opportunity to discuss this proposal further and to provide any additional material that might be helpful to the Executive Committee's consideration.
